My Timeline

2014 - 2018
Data Analyst - Americal Excel Enterprise

I entered the company as a full‑time customer‑service rep, answering every Amazon Seller Central ticket that landed in the queue. Underneath those messages sat a mountain of untapped data—and a storefront stalled at $178 K in monthly sales. Nights and weekends became my personal boot camp: with guidance from our data and software engineers I devoured tutorials and forums, teaching myself Python, SQL, Tableau, R Studio, advanced Microsoft Excel, and even spun up a lightweight CI/CD pipeline on AWS S3 to automate data refreshes.

Clean code turned messy CSVs into reliable tables; those tables became live Tableau dashboards that exposed defect‑rate thresholds, pricing gaps, and replenishment triggers. Each Friday the CEO, the engineers, and I huddled around those dashboards, set one‑week experiments, and watched results roll in. Within a year the same storefront that once crawled at $178 K was clearing $1.2 million a month.

As momentum grew, so did my responsibilities—logistics coordinator before lunch, ad‑hoc graphic designer after, on‑call analyst whenever a red spike flashed on the screen. The company’s cash runway eventually ran out, but the experience rewired my sense of what’s possible: self‑taught grit plus the right tools can turn stagnation into breakout growth. Those skills—and the bias for action they sparked—are the foundation I now bring to every team.

2018 - 2019
Web Developer - JSL Benefits

When I arrived at JSL Benefits, the firm’s online footprint was little more than a placeholder and its “IT department” a tangle of spare cables. Nights of self‑directed tutorials had already armed me with HTML, SCSS, and JavaScript, so I opened a blank editor and started building the site I wished I could visit: fast, responsive, and as reassuring as a firm handshake. Within weeks the new platform was live, complete with secure quote‑request flows that turned browsers into qualified leads. I filmed explainer videos, designed email sequences, and launched social‑media campaigns that lifted us up the search‑rank ladder and made the brand feel as trustworthy online as it was across the conference table.

Behind the scenes, I migrated a patchwork of spreadsheets into PostgreSQL and wrote the SQL routines that cleansed and de‑duplicated every policyholder record, transforming messy data into the engine for renewals and upsells. Between code pushes I kept the office humming—imaging laptops, taming printers, and untangling networks—so the team could stay focused on protecting clients’ peace of mind.

The project proved what self‑taught grit and a clear vision can do, but it also reminded me that unfinished goals have a way of calling you back. When the website was thriving and the infrastructure solid, I stepped away to complete my bachelor’s degree—carrying with me a deeper sense of purpose and a track record of turning blank screens into business results.

2019 - 2022
A.S. Computer Science - Fullerton College

I used to think college was a place you entered only when you already knew who you wanted to be. In 2009 I walked away from community college because I didn’t—and for years I bounced through sales gigs and odd jobs, making every decision alone and rarely asking for help. Then life hit pause: within a single year I lost my closest friend, and my mother. Grief could have closed every door, but instead it pushed one wide open.

I re‑enrolled, this time treating school as a laboratory for reinvention. I sampled everything—automotive technology, electrical work, forensics, nursing—while learning the skills no transcript captures: how to manage time, lead a team, and seek out mentors instead of hiding from them. The spark finally lit in an intro‑to‑programming class. A single missing semicolon kept me and a classmate up until four in the morning, and we loved every minute of the chase. Debugging felt less like homework and more like solving a mystery.

That curiosity became a compass. I dove into C++, led peer study groups, and watched my GPA climb to 3.9. More important than the number was the mindset shift: challenges were no longer threats to my confidence but invitations to grow. I learned to translate setbacks into momentum, to ask for guidance and then offer it, and to see education as a privilege rather than a chore.

Today, whether I’m writing software or mentoring a teammate, I carry the same lesson: our past doesn’t set our ceiling—our willingness to learn does. Returning to college wasn’t a detour; it was the turning point that transformed doubt into direction and loss into purpose.

2023 - 2024
B.S. in Computer Science - California State University of Fullerton

When I enrolled at Cal State Fullerton for my final undergraduate year, more than a few voices—some of them friends and family—told me I didn’t belong in computer science. Schools with more prestiege had sent acceptance letters I couldn’t afford, so I chose the classroom I could reach and decided to wring every drop of value from it.

That meant working two or three jobs while carrying five or six courses a term. I powered through algorithms, data‑structures, operating‑systems, compiler theory, and networking, then piled on advanced probability and statistics because I’d fallen in love with math. A self‑directed deep dive into C showed me how network sockets really work and sharpened the disciplined coding style I now apply in every language. By graduation I could move comfortably between Java, Python, C, SQL, and R; sketch a database schema; reason about runtime; and explain why a p‑value matters as much as a for‑loop.

There were nights I wanted to quit—first‑generation expectations, rent deadlines, and lecture halls that emptied before my shift even started—but resilience became its own muscle. Crossing the stage as the first man in my family to earn a four‑year degree felt less like the end of school and more like proof that grit plus curiosity can outpace circumstance. I still remember seeing a photo of Google’s Santa Monica campus in 2009 and thinking people smarter than me work there. Today I don’t measure myself against someone else’s ceiling; I chase the next problem worth solving, knowing the only limits are the ones I accept.

2022 - 2024
SI Math Instructor - Fullerton College

Fullerton College handed me my first whiteboard and an empty classroom full of possibility. As a Supplemental Instruction Math Instructor I turned lectures in algebra, trigonometry, calculus, and statistics into high‑energy rooms where students solved problems with each other instead of watching me work. Every week I storyboarded lesson plans, wrote Calculus proofs and Statistics formulas, and balanced the room between one‑on‑one coaching and small‑group competitions to make the learning for interactive and engaging.

Friday check‑ins with a circle of veteran SI mentors modeled the feedback loop I try to offer others. I learned to track attendance and quiz scores in Google Sheets, pivot my strategy when a concept didn’t stick, and mediate the occasional “proof‑versus‑calculator” standoff—early lessons in data‑driven teaching, conflict resolution, and peer leadership.

But the most important work happened after the last equation was erased. Students lingered to ask how to juggle a part‑time job, apply for scholarships, or tell their families they wanted to change majors. Those hallway conversations became impromptu workshops on resilience, goal‑setting, and imposter‑syndrome hacks. Mentorship spilled past z‑scores and derivatives into life design, and watching classmates walk into the next semester with new confidence convinced me that technical mastery and human growth are two sides of the same success.

The role braided pedagogy, analytics, and advocacy into one thread, sharpening my ability to translate complex ideas, rally a team around a shared goal, and lead with empathy—talents I now apply whether I will debugging code, mentoring a junior developer, or guiding a project through its first sprint.

2024 - present
Abnormal Behavioral Analyst - First Step Learning

After launching 1,600 job applications into a quiet market and patching together four side hustles—tutoring at dawn, waiting tables at lunch, tossing pizza dough at night, and driving Uber between shifts—I walked into a Cal State Fullerton career fair searching for anything that even smelled like tech. The only booth without a line belonged to a behavioral‑health provider.

“We deliver data‑driven care for children with autism and ADHD,” the recruiter said, describing how therapists record every prompt, response, and pause so Board‑Certified Behavior Analysts can fine‑tune treatment the way an analyst refines a model. My problem‑solver’s brain sparked; if numbers can boost revenue, why not unlock human potential? I signed on a week later.

Day‑to‑day I translate broad treatment goals into measurable trials—guiding a child to request a toy, stretching eye contact by seconds that grow into minutes, coaching parents so progress continues long after I leave the living room. My analytical mindset became an unexpected super‑power: tracking patterns, spotting anomalies early, and turning raw observations into concise reports the BCBA can iterate on the very next day. Patience, polished while running Supplemental‑Instruction math sessions, lets me celebrate inch‑by‑inch victories and keep the room encouraging even when breakthroughs arrive one heartbeat at a time.

Working beside these developing minds also rewired how I understand grown‑up ones. I see how tiny, consistent rewards steer behavior, how attention spikes or fades with subtle cues, and how those same levers drive teamwork and product adoption well beyond childhood. The experience fuses empathy with evidence and confirms a lesson I carry everywhere: careful measurement and human connection are two halves of the same solution.

While I continue to build treatment wins for my clients, I’m still shipping personal coding projects at night and contributing analytics dashboards to local nonprofits—making an impact on my community today while sharpening the skills that will power my next tech role tomorrow.
